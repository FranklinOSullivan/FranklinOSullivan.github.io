Plant Water Sensor

Hardware / Firmware

Purpose

Life is precious. Plants can be espically so as they are able to generate food or other consumable products. All plants require a different level of care. This can be anything from full sunlight all days of the year as is experrienced by desert plants or it could be minimal sunlight with almost no heat allowed, as is often seen in indoor house plants.

In my room. I have a few plants that I care for, mostly for look. My most precious of these is my Adanson's monstera (Monstera Adansonii). This plant comes from the rainforest reigions of Southern and Central America. This means that the plant often requires a great amount of care, as it now lives in a bedroom in New Zealand.

My Adanson's monstera enjoys being difficult. It likes to live in a spot which experiences bright, indirect sunlight, such as a windowsill. It lives best in a temperature range of 18 - 27 Degrees Celcius, with an almost constant humidity level of 60%. This is exptremely difficult to maintain, not only with temperatures in New Zealand bearly fitting the ideal ranges, but also because I have no idea what is going on in the soil or how the plant is feeling on any given day.

I found that the best way to start caring for this plant better would be to start monitoring the soil moisutre content. I achieved this through the use of an HW-080 soil moisture sensor.
I found that if I were to connect the sensor as a variable resistance in a voltage divider with a 10K Ohm resistor, I would be able to see the change in voltage being used by the moisture sensor, and therefore determine the moisture content of the soil.

I took ADC readings from the soil every minute. This was because I found that the moisture content took around 10 days to go from 100% humidity to 0% humidity, and 14400 updates would be pleanty.

I chose to take these readings with an STM32 microcontroller as I found that it would be be a great chance for me to learn about the STM32 better and to deepen my understanding of firmware development in general.

Once the data had been read onto the STM32 microcontroller, I found that I would need to display this data in some way to the user, myself. The easiest and clearest way to achieve this would be through the use of a 7-segment display. When testing if mine still worked, I managed to break it. This meant that I would need to use a different approach. I instead opted to use an I2C Liquid Crystal Display (LCD). This would force me to learn how the I2C protocol works and would allow for a much nicer looking project.

What did I learn?

Thoughout this project I learned many valuable skills. Most importantly was that I learned how to use the I2C protocol. This will greatly increase the scope of projects that I can complete in the future while also allowing me a deeper understanding of the world of electronics around me. Another major learning point for me was that my stuides are paying off and that the choice to use a voltage divider worked very well in describing the humidity of remaining in the soil.

A fact that I learned is that caring for plants is not an exact science and that there is variation in the needs of a plant due to many, many factors. While trying to keep the humidity at 60% was a good idea and would likely have worked in ideal circumstances, I rather needed to keep an average of 60%. This meant allowing the plant time for its roots to dry between waterings, preventing root rot and other forms of sickness.

Future inspiration

This project was good fun as it allowed me not only to care for something that I was passionate about, but it also gave me the chance to expand my electronics and firmware skills into the real world, making a positive difference.

I find that some of the skills that I have gained may be useful in the agriculture and horticulture industries, which are very prominent in New Zealand. I also find that my newly developed firmware skills of using I2C will give me a chance to take on a greater range of projects in the future.

In terms of developing this project further. I hope that in the future I will be able to monitor more factors that influence the plant. This will give me a better understanding of what it needs to survive and how these should be best balanced. There may also be an opportunity to train an AI, which will be able to care for this plant better than a human might be able to.
